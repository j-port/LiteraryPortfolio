Kulakog and Tilmag — A Philippine Myth of Kinship, Sacrifice, and Transformation

Critical Lens: Psychoanalytic Criticism

Psychoanalytic literary criticism applies the theories of Sigmund Freud and his successors (Jacques Lacan, Carl Jung, Melanie Klein) to literature. It explores the unconscious motivations of characters and authors, the symbolic significance of textual elements, and the psychological processes enacted in narrative structures.

Key concepts include the tripartite psyche (id, ego, superego), the Oedipus complex, repression and return of the repressed, defense mechanisms, and dream symbolism. Psychoanalytic critics read texts as if they were dreams—seeking latent meanings beneath manifest content, treating images as symbols of unconscious desires and fears.

"The interpretation of dreams is the royal road to a knowledge of the unconscious activities of the mind." — Sigmund Freud, The Interpretation of Dreams

Summary of the Text

"Kulakog and Tilmag" is a traditional Philippine myth from the Subanon people of Mindanao. It tells the story of two brothers (or in some versions, close kinsmen) whose deep bond is tested by supernatural forces and circumstances beyond their control.

Kulakog and Tilmag live harmoniously until a series of events separates them. In their journey, they encounter various trials including encounters with spirits, tests of loyalty, and situations requiring sacrifice. The narrative explores themes of fraternal devotion, the dangers of the spirit world, and the transformation that comes through suffering.

The story culminates in a moment of profound choice where one brother must decide between self-preservation and sacrificial love. Through their trials, the brothers undergo transformation, ultimately achieving a form of transcendence or spiritual elevation. The myth serves as a meditation on kinship obligations, the nature of sacrifice, and the relationship between the mortal and spirit worlds.

Analysis and Interpretation

The Double and the Divided Self

Psychoanalytically, Kulakog and Tilmag can be read as representing aspects of a single psyche—a common motif in myths and literature. The two brothers embody the duality within the self: perhaps ego and id, conscious and unconscious, or social self and shadow self. Their narrative journey represents the psyche's struggle toward integration.

The "double" or "doppelgänger" motif, extensively analyzed by Otto Rank and Freud, represents the return of repressed aspects of self. In this reading, the brothers' separation and reunion enact the psychodynamic process of splitting and integration—the ego's recognition of and reconciliation with its repressed or disowned parts.

The Id and the Spirit World

The spirit world encountered by the brothers can be understood as a representation of the unconscious. In Freudian topography, the unconscious is populated by repressed desires, fears, and primitive impulses—much like the spirit world is populated by entities that operate outside human social norms and rational control.

The dangers the brothers face in the spirit realm represent the threats posed by uncontrolled unconscious content. The spirits they encounter may symbolize repressed desires (the id's demands), internalized authority figures (superego manifestations), or traumatic memories seeking expression. Navigating this realm requires the ego's resources of reason, courage, and adaptation.

"Where id was, there ego shall be."

— Sigmund Freud

Sacrifice and the Death Drive

Freud's concept of the death drive (Thanatos), introduced in "Beyond the Pleasure Principle," suggests a fundamental tendency toward dissolution, return to an inorganic state, and self-destruction alongside the life drives (Eros). The sacrificial element in the myth—one brother giving up something essential for the other—can be read through this lens.

The willingness to sacrifice demonstrates the death drive's sublimation into heroic self-abnegation. Rather than destructive, this sacrifice becomes creative, transformative, even life-giving. The myth suggests that confronting and channeling the death drive—acknowledging the necessity of loss and endings—enables psychological and spiritual growth.

The Family Romance and Kinship Bonds

The intense bond between Kulakog and Tilmag evokes what Freud called the "family romance"—the complex web of desire, rivalry, and identification within family structures. The brothers' relationship, while presented positively, carries undertones of ambivalence characteristic of sibling bonds: love intertwined with competition, devotion shadowed by jealousy.

The trials that test their bond can be read as manifestations of this ambivalence—externalized projections of internal conflicts. By successfully navigating these trials, the brothers work through their ambivalence, achieving a purified bond freed from destructive undercurrents.

Transformation and Individuation

Drawing on Carl Jung's concept of individuation—the process of integrating conscious and unconscious elements to achieve psychological wholeness—we can read the brothers' transformation as a mythic representation of this process. Their journey through trials, encounter with the spirit world, and ultimate transcendence mirror the stages of individuation.

The myth's resolution, where the brothers achieve a new state of being, represents the goal of individuation: the Self as a totality greater than the ego alone. The narrative provides a cultural template for the individuation process, offering its audience symbolic guidance for their own psychological development.

Conclusion

Psychoanalytic reading reveals "Kulakog and Tilmag" as a sophisticated narrative of psychological dynamics—the struggle between drives, the confrontation with the unconscious, and the journey toward integration. The brothers represent not merely external characters but internal psychic forces whose relationship determines mental health or illness, stagnation or growth.

This analysis demonstrates how indigenous myths carry profound psychological wisdom that psychoanalytic theory helps articulate. Far from being "primitive" tales, such narratives represent humanity's ongoing attempt to understand and guide the soul's journey through life's challenges toward wholeness. The Subanon myth, like dreams Freud analyzed, provides a royal road to understanding the deep structures of human psychological experience.
