Course Overview

Throughout this Great Books course, I have journeyed through six distinct literary analyses, each employing a different critical lens to illuminate timeless texts. From ancient Indian fables to American Southern Gothic, from classical mythology to Philippine folklore, these works span millennia and continents yet share fundamental concerns about human nature, society, and meaning.

The application of diverse critical approaches—Archetypal, Reader-Response, Feminist, Queer Theory, Formalist, and Psychoanalytic—has revealed how the same text can yield vastly different insights depending on the questions we bring to it. No single approach exhausts a text's meaning; rather, each opens windows onto particular dimensions of literary experience.

Key Themes Across Works

Identity & Transformation

From Iphis's bodily transformation to Emily's psychological metamorphosis, from heroic archetypes to the doubled brothers—questions of who we are and who we might become pervade these texts.

Power & Resistance

Patriarchal structures in Cupid and Psyche, class dynamics in Faulkner, divine authority throughout—literature consistently examines how power operates and how individuals navigate or resist it.

Love & Connection

Whether romantic (Iphis and Ianthe), fraternal (Kulakog and Tilmag), or perverted (Emily and Homer), human bonds and their complexities form the emotional core of narrative meaning.

Wisdom & Knowledge

The Panchatantra's practical wisdom, Psyche's forbidden knowledge, formalism's textual analysis—literature serves as a vehicle for understanding, even when that knowledge comes at a cost.

The Unconscious & Shadow

Repressed desires in Emily's sealed room, the spirit world as unconscious realm, archetypal shadows—texts reveal what conscious minds prefer to hide.

Time & Memory

Faulkner's non-linear structure, myth's eternal recurrence, the reader's changing relationship with texts—literature exists in and manipulates our experience of temporality.

The Six Critical Lenses

I

Archetypal Criticism

Exemplar Heroicum — Applied to a contemporary film/series

Revealed universal patterns (hero, shadow, mentor) that connect modern narratives to ancient mythological structures, demonstrating the persistence of collective unconscious imagery.

II

Reader-Response Criticism

Responsio Lectoris — Applied to the Panchatantra

Demonstrated how meaning emerges from the transaction between reader and text, with personal experience shaping interpretation of ancient wisdom tales.

III

Feminist Criticism

Vox Feminae — Applied to Cupid and Psyche

Exposed the politics of beauty, the constraints and possibilities of female agency, and the complex dynamics of power in gender relations within classical mythology.

IV

Queer Theory

Spectrum Amoris — Applied to Iphis and Ianthe

Challenged heteronormative assumptions, revealing the instability of gender and sexuality categories and the presence of diverse desires in classical texts.

V

Formalism

Forma Perfecta — Applied to "A Rose for Emily"

Analyzed how structure, narrative technique, symbolism, and literary devices create meaning, demonstrating the inseparability of form and content.

VI

Psychoanalytic Criticism

Anima Profunda — Applied to Kulakog and Tilmag

Explored the unconscious dynamics of Philippine myth—the double, the death drive, family romance—revealing psychological wisdom in indigenous narrative.

Collective Insights

"Literature is the most agreeable way of ignoring life." — Fernando Pessoa

Yet paradoxically, through this "ignoring," we often see life more clearly.

The Value of Multiple Perspectives

The most significant insight from this course is the necessity of multiple critical approaches. No single lens captures the full complexity of literary experience. Archetypal criticism reveals patterns; Reader-Response personalizes meaning; Feminist and Queer Theory expose power and identity politics; Formalism honors craft; Psychoanalytic criticism plumbs depths. Together, they create a richer, more complete understanding than any alone could provide.

Literature as Cultural Mirror

These texts, spanning diverse cultures and eras, consistently reflect fundamental human concerns—love, death, identity, power, meaning. The Great Books are "great" not because of some inherent superiority but because they articulate experiences that transcend their original contexts, speaking to readers across time and place.

Reading as Active Practice

Perhaps most importantly, this course has demonstrated that reading is not passive reception but active construction. The reader brings questions, experiences, and frameworks that shape what the text becomes. Literary analysis is not about finding predetermined meanings but about engaging in creative dialogue with texts, generating insights that illuminate both literature and life.

"The unexamined life is not worth living."

— Socrates

The unexamined text, we might add, is not fully read.
